Illuminating Worlds

Lighting in Video Games and Architecture

Begin Journey

Overview and Objectives

Lighting is a fundamental creative tool in both video game design and architectural design. In each domain, it is used not only for visibility but to shape how spaces feel and how people navigate and interpret those spaces.

🎨 Creative Expression

Modern designers recognize that lighting powerfully influences human experience -- from our emotions and mood to our sense of orientation.

🎮 Game Development

Game developers know that strategic lighting can guide players and enrich storytelling in a virtual world.

🏗️ Architecture

Architects use lighting to create atmospheres, guide movement, and tell stories through the built environment.

Structure

The syllabus is divided into four main sessions plus an introduction and conclusion. Each session includes clear learning objectives, key discussion points, real-world and game case studies (with visuals where appropriate), and interactive elements or prompts. The aim is to engage architects, interior designers, and construction professionals in dialogue with concepts from video game lighting design, highlighting cross-disciplinary insights.

Session 1: Light and Mood -- Setting the Atmosphere

Understanding how lighting establishes mood and atmosphere in both virtual and physical spaces

Learning Objectives

  • Identify how lighting establishes mood and atmosphere in both a video game scene and a physical space.
  • Understand the effects of color temperature, light intensity, and contrast on human emotion and perception in an environment.
  • Compare techniques used by game artists and architects to evoke feelings of peace, warmth, tension, or mystery through lighting design.

Key Discussion Points

Color and Emotion

How warm vs. cool light colors influence mood. Warm golden lighting evokes comfort or excitement, whereas cool blue lighting feels calm or even somber. Discussion will include color psychology in interiors (such as warm lighting in restaurants for intimacy vs. cool lighting in hospitals for alertness) and analogous uses in games (a sunny Mario level vs. a cold blue mystery puzzle level).

Brightness and Contrast

How brightness levels and shadows create ambience. Well-lit open spaces often feel safe or uplifting, whereas dim, shadowy environments add drama or suspense. In games, a bright meadow scene can convey tranquility, while a dark corridor builds tension. Similarly, in architecture, a sun-filled atrium might feel tranquil, while a low-lit narrow hallway can induce a sense of mystery or anxiety.

Architectural Atmosphere

Review of real projects where lighting "makes" the space. For instance, examine a tranquil spa or library with soft diffuse daylight versus a nightclub or theater lobby with bold, colored accent lights. How do these choices support the intended use and feeling of the space?

Game Environment Atmosphere

Case examples from games known for mood lighting -- e.g. the haunting, low-light atmosphere of Dark Souls or the vibrant, glowing vistas of Cyberpunk 2077. What feelings do these lighting styles create, and why? How do game artists use techniques like fog, backlight, or color grading to amplify mood?

Session 2: Guiding the Gaze -- Lighting for Orientation and Wayfinding

Understanding how designers use lighting as a wayfinding and guidance tool in both virtual levels and physical buildings

Learning Objectives

  • Understand how designers use lighting as a wayfinding and guidance tool in both virtual levels and physical buildings.
  • Learn techniques to direct attention or movement using focal light, contrast, and visual cues.
  • Explore the subconscious influence of light on where people look and how they navigate, drawing parallels between a player's journey through a game level and a person's path through a building.

Key Discussion Points

Attention and Focal Points

Using light to highlight what's important. In architecture, lighting designers often accentuate key features (like an entrance, an exit sign, or a reception desk) with brighter or focused lighting, creating a visual hierarchy in the space. In games, level designers do the same -- a ray of light might fall on a door or item that the player needs to find, or a brightly lit area indicates the correct path amidst darker surroundings.

Wayfinding and Navigation

How lighting "leads" people along. Humans are inherently drawn toward illuminated areas. We will look at design studies (e.g. a Clemson University experiment) showing that people will intuitively walk toward a lit corridor over a dark one, even against signage. In architecture, this principle is used in corridor lighting, path lights, and atrium designs that draw visitors deeper into a space. In games, think of how Portal 2 uses a consistent lighting and color scheme to signal portals and exits -- players subconsciously follow the light and color cues to solve puzzles.

Spatial Orientation

Lighting helps people understand space and position. Discuss how shadows and highlights can reveal spatial depth -- in a game, dynamic lighting and shadows give clues about the geometry of the environment (e.g. distinguishing a platform's edge), while in architecture, good lighting ensures stairs, ramps, or obstacles are perceptible for safety and comfort.

Reducing Visual Clutter

Emphasize how intentional lighting design can reduce reliance on signs or on-screen HUD indicators. When light is used smartly, fewer arrows or signs are needed -- the environment itself tells you where to go. Architects often strive for "intuitive wayfinding" in which lighting plays a part (e.g. a naturally bright atrium signals the main gathering space). Game designers similarly aim for diegetic cues -- using environmental lights instead of UI markers to guide players.

Case 3 -- Portal 2's Test Chambers

In Portal 2, players navigate complex puzzle rooms. One way the game subtly guides players is through lighting and color -- important surfaces and exits are well-lit or painted in high-contrast colors, while unimportant areas remain dim. This provides "unconscious" direction for the player. We will show a screenshot of a Portal 2 chamber, noting how a brightly lit doorway naturally attracts the eye.

Case 4 -- Hospital Corridor Wayfinding

A segment from a healthcare design where lighting was critical to wayfinding -- for example, an LED-lit feature wall at the end of a long corridor in a hospital that draws visitors forward, or the use of colored lighting in different wings to differentiate them. This illustrates replacing or augmenting signage with lighting cues (a chandelier or glowing sculpture says "come this way" without a sign reading "Lobby here").

Cross-Comparison

We explicitly compare the above two cases: What similarities exist between how Portal 2 and a hospital lobby use lighting to direct people? Participants might note the emphasis on clarity and reducing confusion in both. We also touch on constraints: unlike games, real buildings have to consider glare and comfort (too much brightness can physically hurt the eyes), whereas in a game a dramatic lens flare might be acceptable or even exciting.

Interactive Element: "Light the Path" Group Discussion

We present a simple floor plan of a public space (for instance, a small museum or an outdoor plaza). The task for participants is to propose a lighting strategy that guides a first-time visitor through this space from entrance to exit, highlighting key points of interest.

Session 3: Storytelling with Light -- Narrative and Emotion

Exploring how lighting can serve as a storytelling device in both interactive virtual worlds and static architectural spaces

Learning Objectives

  • Explore how lighting can serve as a storytelling device in both interactive virtual worlds and static architectural spaces.
  • Learn about techniques like dynamic lighting changes, thematic lighting, and symbolic use of light and shadow that convey narrative or thematic content.
  • Analyze parallels between theatrical/stage lighting for drama and how architects and game designers alike use lighting to support a story or message in the environment.

Key Discussion Points

Environmental Storytelling

Both architects and game designers use lighting to imply backstory or narrative context without words. In games, a flickering neon sign in an alley suggests a city's decay or a lurking danger, whereas a shaft of light breaking through a dilapidated roof can symbolize hope in a narrative. In architecture, consider how the lighting of a memorial at night (e.g. two vertical beams of light at the 9/11 Memorial in NYC) tells a story of remembrance, or how an old cathedral's dim, colored light through stained glass conveys a sense of sacred history.

Dynamic Lighting and Progression

In narratives, lighting often changes to reflect story progression. We see this in theater and films through lighting cues for different acts or emotional beats. Games borrow this idea: as a story level reaches a climax, the lighting might shift (a storm rolls in, lights flicker, or color grading turns red for danger). Architects can also design dynamic lighting into spaces -- e.g. a restaurant that gradually dims lights as the evening progresses to transform the atmosphere, or an interactive installation that changes color based on visitor activity.

Theatrical Techniques in Architecture

Drawing from stage and film, architectural lighting designers use intensity, color, angle, and movement to dramatize a space. We will break down a few theatrical lighting concepts: for example, gobos projecting patterns of light and shadow (used in theater to simulate dappled foliage or stained glass effects) and see how an architect might use patterned screens or filtered daylight for a similar storytelling effect in a space.

Symbolism and Meaning

Sometimes light itself carries metaphor or symbolism. In sacred architecture, the interplay of light and dark often represents spiritual themes (light = divine presence, etc.). We'll see how Tadao Ando's Church of Light embodies the concept of duality (material vs. immaterial, light vs. dark as metaphors for spiritual enlightenment) -- a reminder that architects intentionally design lighting not just for function but to support the story or concept of the space.

Case Studies & Visual Examples

Tadao Ando's Church of Light, Osaka

A powerful architectural example of storytelling through natural light. A cross-shaped aperture in the concrete wall projects a brilliant cruciform of light that moves across the darkened chapel as the sun shifts. This dramatic contrast of light and shadow is central to the church's narrative experience, turning the interior into an ever-changing canvas of illumination. The design conveys a metaphor -- the interpenetration of the spiritual (light) into the material (concrete darkness) -- allowing visitors to feel a story of faith and contemplation without any words.

Session 4: Cross-Inspiration and Synthesis -- Bridging Game and Architectural Lighting

Synthesizing insights to understand how architects and game designers can learn from each other's approach to lighting

Learning Objectives

  • Synthesize the insights from earlier sessions to understand how architects and game designers can learn from each other's approach to lighting.
  • Recognize both the constraints and freedoms of each medium: what can be done in games that real architecture can adapt conceptually, and how real-world lighting considerations (human comfort, physical materials) can inform game lighting for realism and relatability.
  • Brainstorm innovative ideas by combining best practices from both fields, and consider future trends (dynamic, interactive lighting, virtual reality) impacting lighting design.

Key Discussion Points

Comparing Constraints

We start by acknowledging differences -- in architecture, lighting must obey physics and meet practical requirements (energy codes, maintenance, occupant comfort), whereas in video games lighting can break physical rules for the sake of art or gameplay. For example, in a game you might have a light with no visible source or ignore gravity and power sources, something impossible in a real building. Conversely, architects deal with long-term experiences (people live and work under these lights daily), while game lighting is experienced for short play sessions.

Lessons from Game Lighting for Architects

Immersion and Experimentation: Game worlds often have wildly imaginative lighting (otherworldly colors, "impossible" scenarios like floating lights or interactive day-night cycles). While not all of that translates directly, it can inspire architects to experiment with bolder lighting designs in themed environments, exhibitions, or experiential spaces.

Pre-visualization and Simulation: Game development pipelines use powerful real-time engines to test lighting iteratively. Architects can borrow this practice -- using game engines or VR simulations to visualize and play with lighting in a virtual model of a building.

Environmental Storytelling in Placemaking: Video games excel at making environments that convey lore and invite exploration. Architects and urban designers aiming to create memorable places (public squares, themed restaurants, hotels) can take cues from how games use lighting to define a strong sense of place or identity.

Lessons from Architectural Lighting for Game Designers

Human-Centric Design: Architects increasingly design lighting for occupant well-being, considering factors like circadian rhythms, glare reduction, and psychological comfort. Game lighting artists could incorporate some of these principles to enhance player comfort and realism.

Technical Collaboration: In building projects, lighting designers work closely with architects, electrical engineers, etc., balancing aesthetics with technical feasibility. In game production, lighting artists can similarly collaborate with engine programmers and level designers to ensure their dramatic lighting doesn't impede gameplay.

Sustainable & Adaptive Lighting: A growing trend in architecture is sustainable lighting -- using smart controls, daylight harvesting, etc. While games don't have an energy bill, the concept of efficient lighting aligns with optimizing performance (rendering fewer light sources saves computation).

Future Trends -- Convergence of Real and Virtual

We conclude this session by looking at areas where architectural and game lighting are directly overlapping. For example, Augmented Reality (AR) and Virtual Reality (VR) are being used by architects to create virtual spaces and by game designers to bring game worlds into the real environment. The cross-pollination potential is huge, and staying open to ideas from each other's discipline will be a competitive advantage.

Conclusion and Key Takeaways

Lighting is a universal language in environmental design, whether the environment is physical or virtual

Core Message

By examining lighting through both the architect's and the game designer's eyes, we gain a richer toolkit for shaping experiences. Key takeaways include:

🎭 Storytelling & Mood

In both architecture and video games, lighting is not just illumination but a storyteller, a guide, and a mood setter. It subtly influences how people feel and behave in a space. Good lighting design in any context requires thinking about the human experience at the center.

🧭 Orientation & Focus

Light naturally draws the eye, so use it deliberately. Whether you want a player to find a hidden path or a visitor to notice an emergency exit, placing light strategically can achieve that without words. Both fields benefit from remembering that often "less is more" -- a single well-placed light can replace a dozen signs or instructions.

🎨 Narrative & Theme

Thematic lighting unifies design. A strong concept for lighting (e.g. "dappled forest light" or "neon dystopia") can inform everything from fixture choice to color palette. Embrace lighting as an expressive medium: architects can borrow theatrical drama to make spaces memorable, and game creators can ground fantastical scenes with architectural realism for authenticity.

🤝 Collaboration & Experimentation

Perhaps most importantly, architects, interior designers, and game artists should continue to look outside their immediate field for inspiration. An architect might play a critically acclaimed video game to spark ideas for experiential lighting, while a game artist might study architectural masterpieces for lessons in timeless, elegant lighting design.

Final Challenge

Finally, we encourage all participants to apply their new insights to their upcoming projects. Whether designing a building or a level, ask yourself: "What emotion or story do I want to tell with the lighting here?" and "How can light guide the users or players through the experience?"

Discussion Prompt (Post-Presentation): Consider one space (virtual or real) that left a strong impression on you because of its lighting. What did you feel in that space, and what lighting choices contributed to it?
